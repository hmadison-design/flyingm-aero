The Argument, Made Concrete

A thesis.
Proven.
In use.

The aviation industry has two problems that look separate but aren't. Too many pilots are getting hurt. And not enough pilots are being made. Both problems have the same root: flight training that is too slow, too expensive, and too inconsistent to produce pilots at the scale and quality the industry needs.

Harvey's answer has been to build learning systems that change that equation — simulator-based, standards-driven, and designed around how psychomotor skills are actually acquired. When a learner arrives at the airplane having already practiced a maneuver to ACS standards in a simulator, they are 90% of the way to proficiency before the engine starts. That changes everything: the speed of learning, the cost of training, the pass rate, the retention rate — and ultimately, the safety record of the pilot who graduates.

This isn't a replacement for CFIs. It's what makes CFIs more effective. Students arrive prepared. Airplane time is spent on refinement, not introduction. Instructors log more quality hours, schools retain more students, and the pipeline produces more pilots — safer ones, faster.

Primary Training · 2015
GIFT‑PPL
Guided Independent Flight Training — Private Pilot
33 modules · One cohesive system · A decade in active use

GIFT guides learners through every maneuver and procedure required for private pilot certification — in a simulator, to ACS standards, independently. Before they ever fly the maneuver with an instructor, they've already practiced it. They arrive at the airplane prepared, not introduced.

The result is measurably faster skill acquisition, lower training costs, higher first-attempt checkride pass rates, and better student retention — because students who progress don't quit. CFIs get students who are ready to refine, not students who are starting from zero. Everyone wins, especially the pilot who graduates safer and sooner.

Instrument Training · 2017
GIFT‑IR
Guided Independent Flight Training — Instrument Rating
10 modules · The rating that saves lives

The data on this is unambiguous: instrument-rated pilots survive situations that kill VFR-only pilots. Inadvertent IMC entry is one of general aviation's most reliably fatal accident categories — and the instrument rating is its antidote. Getting more pilots rated, and getting them rated correctly, is one of the highest-leverage interventions available to anyone serious about the GA accident rate.

GIFT-IR applies the same instructional architecture as GIFT-PPL to IFR procedures, approaches, and the cognitive demands of flight in IMC — so learners arrive at the airplane having already practiced the procedures, not encountering them for the first time. Same mechanism, higher stakes, and an argument for the instrument rating that goes beyond currency or convenience.

Second Opinion iOS · iPad

Before you fly, are you actually fit to fly? Second Opinion is a pilot self-assessment app that combines three quick cognitive and reflex acuity tests with a structured Flight Risk Assessment Tool — giving pilots a data-informed answer to the question every preflight briefing should include but rarely does. Built on the premise that the most dangerous variable in any flight is the one sitting in the left seat, and that pilots deserve a tool that takes that seriously.

The Person Behind the Work

Harvey Madison has spent his career at an unusual intersection: the science of how people learn complex skills, the operational realities of flying general aviation aircraft, and the institutional challenge of actually getting better training into the hands of working pilots.

He is a pilot himself — which matters. The systems he designs aren't built from a distance. They're built by someone who has sat in the left seat, felt the workload, made the decisions, and understands what it costs when pilots aren't prepared.

The Webster Trophy calls him an Honorary Canadian. The FAA has certified him as a Training Provider. AOPA, Flying, AVweb, and Plane & Pilot have featured his work. None of that is what gets him up in the morning.

What gets him up is the accident rate — and the conviction that it doesn't have to be what it is.
